Hospital Stocks Are Down 21% — The Larger Medicaid Shock Hits in 31 Days

Hospital operators have already absorbed a $1.25 billion hit from expired ACA subsidies and kept guidance, but Medicaid work requirements taking effect July 31 represent a second and larger revenue shock that analyst estimates have not yet incorporated.
By Alessio Cipriano  ·  June 30, 2026  ·  9:10 AM ET

The Guidance That Masks a Second Floor

HCA Healthcare and Tenet Healthcare each reaffirmed their full-year 2026 earnings guidance in May, after absorbing a combined $1.25 billion in headwinds from expired ACA marketplace subsidies.

The market read that as resilience. It isn't.

The two companies held guidance because they had already modeled the ACA subsidy shock. The Medicaid work requirements signed into law July 4, 2025, which take effect July 31, 2026, represent a different and larger revenue event. Neither company has issued guidance incorporating the post-July 31 enrollment losses, because those losses land in Q3 and Q4, the quarters that analysts have not yet revised.

Two Shocks, Two Separate Mechanisms

Enhanced ACA marketplace subsidies expired at the end of 2025. Those subsidies had kept premiums low enough that roughly 23 million people could afford marketplace plans. When they lapsed, enrollment fell. HCA reported a 15% decline in exchange-based admissions in Q1 2026 versus the year-earlier period. Uninsured admissions rose 16% in the same comparison. HCA estimated its full-year ACA exposure at up to $1 billion. Tenet put its figure at $250 million for 2026.

Medicaid work requirements operate through a different channel. Rather than pricing people out of coverage, they require adults aged 18 to 64 to document 80 hours of monthly work or community service to retain Medicaid enrollment. The requirement is administrative, not income-based. People who meet the income threshold to qualify for Medicaid can still lose coverage if they fail to file the right paperwork on time or in the correct format.

The Congressional Budget Office estimated the work requirement provisions will reduce federal Medicaid spending by $326 billion over 10 years. The Urban Institute projects 5.3 million more people will be uninsured by 2034 as a result.

The Uncompensated Care Cascade

Medicaid covers approximately 19% of all U.S. hospital admissions. When Medicaid patients lose coverage and still need care, they arrive as uninsured patients. Hospitals must treat them under the Emergency Medical Treatment and Labor Act regardless of ability to pay. Most of those costs become uncompensated care, absorbed by the hospital's operating margin.

The Robert Wood Johnson Foundation estimated that if states drop Medicaid expansion, hospitals would face $31.9 billion in annual revenue losses and $19 billion in increased uncompensated care costs. Safety-net hospitals, which depend on Medicaid for an average of 24% of net patient revenues, face the largest direct exposure.

The ACA subsidy lapse previewed this cascade at smaller scale. HCA's 16% rise in uninsured admissions in Q1 came alongside a 15% drop in exchange-insured patients. The same pattern repeats when Medicaid patients lose coverage under work requirements. They still arrive. The revenue does not follow.

Where the Losses Land on Equity

The four publicly traded hospital chains most directly exposed are HCA Healthcare, Tenet Healthcare, Community Health Systems, and Universal Health Services. Together they operate roughly 750 hospitals and employ several hundred thousand workers.

HCA is down 21% year to date. Universal Health Services is down 35.3%. Both declines reflect the ACA subsidy shock. The Medicaid work requirement enrollment losses do not begin until August at the earliest and will not appear in reported earnings until Q3.

Community Health Systems, which the Zacks consensus projects will report a loss of $0.58 per share in 2026, carries no margin buffer for additional revenue pressure. Its stock cannot absorb a second proportional decline on top of what it has already given up.

The floor is lower still for rural hospitals, which work requirements affect most directly because rural Medicaid patients are less likely to have employers who can provide documentation support. The average rural hospital operating margin is 3.1%, and 44% of rural hospitals are already operating with negative margins. For those facilities, enrollment churn is not a profitability problem. It is a closure trigger.

What Breaks This Thesis

Courts have blocked Medicaid work requirements before. The U.S. Court of Appeals for the D.C. Circuit struck down Arkansas's 2018 implementation, and legal challenges to the current provisions are already filed. If a federal court issues a preliminary injunction before July 31, the enrollment losses do not materialize on the current schedule.

States may also partially absorb the impact through supplemental Medicaid payment programs. Earlier in 2026, HCA's supplemental income came in at $200 million against an $80 million expectation, as state programs reinstated payments to offset federal cuts. That cushion helped HCA hold Q1 guidance. It could partially repeat.

If both court action and state supplements materialize at scale, the revenue hit shrinks. The CBO's $326 billion projection assumes full implementation across all states. A court-delayed rollout with active state supplemental programs produces a slower, smaller drawdown. The thesis weakens proportionally.

Closing Thoughts

Hospital stocks have already fallen 21% and 35% as the ACA subsidy shock worked through their payer mix. The Medicaid work requirements add a structurally larger pressure on July 31, and Q3 earnings will be the first reporting period to show its scope.

The market priced the first floor. The second one is being laid right now.

Plain English

Even though major hospital company stocks have already fallen more than 20 percent this year, the government policy that will hurt them most hasn't started yet. I argue this because a law signed last year requires people who have Medicaid health coverage to prove they are working at least 80 hours a month, starting July 31, and millions of people are expected to lose their coverage simply because they can't navigate the paperwork, even if they technically still qualify based on their income. Although hospitals have already been dealing with a separate hit from expired subsidies that helped people afford private insurance, the Medicaid work requirement is a different and larger problem that hasn't yet shown up in the companies' quarterly earnings. When patients lose their Medicaid coverage and still need care, hospitals still have to treat them but receive very little payment, so you may start seeing smaller hospitals in your area cutting services or closing their doors in the months ahead.
